## 4.1 Opening: When a brand name becomes a verb

In 1903, the Bayer pharmaceutical company in Germany filed for and received a trademark on a new product: a synthetic over-the-counter pain reliever they had just begun selling in the United States. The trademark name was *Aspirin* — a coined word, derived from "acetyl" plus "spir" (for the spiraea plant from which a related compound had been isolated) plus "in" (a chemical naming convention). For nearly two decades, *Aspirin* was a Bayer brand. Pharmacists who sold a different manufacturer's acetylsalicylic acid had to call the product something else.

In 1921, in *Bayer v. United Drug Co.*, a federal court found that the word *Aspirin* had become *generic* — that to ordinary consumers, the term no longer identified Bayer's particular product but had come to denote acetylsalicylic acid generally. The trademark was canceled. After 1921, anyone could sell pain-reliever tablets under the name "aspirin" in the United States; the name was now a category descriptor, not a brand identifier. Bayer lost the trademark not because of any failure of marketing — Bayer had marketed *Aspirin* aggressively — but because the marketing had been *too* successful. The name had escaped the brand and become the thing.

This is *genericide*. Other casualties have included escalator (originally an Otis Elevator trademark, declared generic in 1950), thermos (originally a King-Seeley Thermos brand, declared generic in 1963), zipper (B.F. Goodrich, declared generic decades after the founding patent expired). Cellophane, kerosene, linoleum, dry ice, heroin — all started as proprietary brand names; all were lost to genericide.

The principle is structural. A trademark exists to identify the *source* of goods and services. When a name no longer performs that source-identifying function — when it has become the name of the *thing*, not the *brand* of the thing — the legal protection collapses. Trademarks are different from patents and copyrights in this sense: they are tied to active commercial use as a source identifier, and they require active enforcement to maintain. A trademark holder who fails to police use of the mark, who allows the term to drift into generic usage, can lose the mark entirely.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- *State* what a trademark is and what it protects.
- *Distinguish* the spectrum of trademark distinctiveness: generic, descriptive, suggestive, arbitrary, fanciful.
- *Explain* the *likelihood-of-confusion* test for trademark infringement.
- *Identify* trademark dilution and recognize when it applies.
- *Apply* the trademark registration process, including the difference between registered (®) and unregistered (™) marks.
- *Recognize* the indefinite duration of trademark protection — and what threatens it.

## 4.2 What a trademark is

A trademark is a word, phrase, symbol, design, or combination thereof used in commerce to identify and distinguish the *source* of goods or services. Examples that need no introduction: the Nike swoosh; the Coca-Cola script; the Apple bitten-apple logo; the McDonald's golden arches; the names *Nike*, *Coca-Cola*, *Apple*, *McDonald's* themselves.

Two related categories:

*Service marks* — trademarks for services rather than goods. The Federal Express tagline "When it absolutely, positively has to be there overnight" was a service mark. The legal treatment is essentially identical; the term "trademark" is often used generically to cover both.

*Trade dress* — the overall visual appearance of a product or its packaging that identifies the source. The shape of a Coca-Cola contour bottle. The interior design of an Apple Store. The orange-and-blue color scheme of a Tide detergent box. Trade dress is protected when it has become *distinctive* — when consumers recognize it as a source identifier.

The basic legal framework is the *Lanham Act* of 1946 (15 U.S.C. § 1051 et seq.), which created the federal trademark registration system administered by the USPTO and provided federal causes of action for infringement. State trademark law also exists, and trademark rights can arise from common-law use even without federal registration.

Trademark law's central goal is *consumer protection* — preventing consumer confusion about the source of goods or services. A buyer who sees the Nike swoosh on a shoe expects a Nike-quality product. A counterfeit Nike shoe with a faked swoosh defeats that expectation. The legal remedy operates on the consumer-confusion theory: the counterfeiter is liable not (primarily) because Nike has been wronged, but because consumers have been confused.

A secondary goal is protecting the trademark holder's investment in brand-building. Companies that spend decades and billions of dollars building a brand have a legitimate interest in not seeing that investment captured by free riders. The two goals — consumer protection and investment protection — usually align, but they can diverge in unusual cases (e.g., genuine non-confusing parodies, where consumer protection is satisfied but investment protection is arguably not).

## 4.3 The distinctiveness spectrum

Not every word can become a trademark. Trademark law sorts terms along a *spectrum of distinctiveness* that determines whether — and how easily — protection is available. The five categories, from least to most protectable:

*Generic* — terms that name the category itself. "Phone" for telephones. "Bicycle" for bicycles. "Aspirin" (post-genericide) for acetylsalicylic acid. Generic terms can never function as trademarks because they cannot distinguish one source from another.

*Descriptive* — terms that describe a quality, feature, or characteristic of the product. "Cold and Creamy" for ice cream. "Sharp" for knives. Descriptive terms are *not* protectable as trademarks unless they have acquired *secondary meaning* — consumer recognition that the term has come to identify a specific source. "Sharp Electronics" can be a protected trademark for consumer electronics because consumers recognize "Sharp" as referring to a specific company, even though the word itself is descriptive of knives.

*Suggestive* — terms that suggest a quality without describing it directly, requiring some imagination to connect to the product. "Greyhound" for buses (suggesting speed). "Citibank" (city + bank, hinting at urban banking). "Coppertone" for sunscreen. Suggestive marks are protectable without proof of secondary meaning.

*Arbitrary* — common words used in ways unrelated to the product. "Apple" for computers. "Camel" for cigarettes. "Shell" for gasoline. The words have everyday meanings; the trademark is the unusual application of the word to an unrelated product. Arbitrary marks are strongly protectable.

*Fanciful* (or *coined*) — invented words with no prior meaning. "Kodak." "Xerox." "Exxon." "Verizon." Fanciful marks are the strongest, because they are unmistakably source-identifying — there is no other meaning the word could have.

The trade-off across the spectrum: generic and descriptive terms buy *immediate consumer comprehensibility* (everyone knows what an "ice cream sandwich" is) at the cost of *no trademark protection*. Fanciful terms buy *strong protection* at the cost of *requiring marketing investment to teach consumers what the word means*. Arbitrary and suggestive marks sit in the middle. Most successful brands sit at the suggestive-to-fanciful end of the spectrum; brands at the descriptive end fight harder for protection and are more vulnerable to genericide.

## 4.4 Acquiring trademark rights

Two paths to trademark rights in the U.S.

*Common-law rights from use*. The mere act of using a mark in commerce, in connection with goods or services, creates common-law trademark rights in the geographic area of use. No registration required. These rights are limited to the actual area of use and to closely related goods/services. A coffee shop named "Starbucks" operating only in one neighborhood would have common-law rights in that neighborhood.

*Federal registration through the USPTO*. The Lanham Act allows a trademark owner to register the mark, gaining substantial benefits beyond common-law rights:

- *Nationwide priority* — registration provides constructive notice to everyone in the U.S. that the mark is claimed.
- *Presumption of validity* — registered marks are presumed valid; challengers bear the burden of proof.
- *Right to use the ®* symbol (which signals registration; ™ signals an unregistered claim).
- *Statutory damages and attorney's fees* in some infringement cases.
- *Customs Service enforcement* against counterfeit imports.
- *Incontestability* after five years of continuous use post-registration (the registration becomes harder to challenge on most grounds).

The registration process. The applicant files an application identifying the mark, the goods/services, and either actual use of the mark or a *bona fide intent to use*. The USPTO examines the application — checking for descriptiveness, conflicts with prior registered marks, and procedural defects. Approved applications are *published for opposition*; third parties have 30 days to file an opposition. Unopposed applications proceed to registration; opposed applications go through an adversarial proceeding before the *Trademark Trial and Appeal Board* (TTAB).

International registration follows separate paths. The *Madrid Protocol* (which the U.S. joined in 2003) allows a single international application that designates protection in member countries. National registrations are still required in non-Madrid countries.

The trade-off in registration: it buys *strong nationwide rights with administrative remedies* at the cost of *examination expense and the risk of opposition*. Most commercially significant brands register; small businesses often rely on common-law rights for years before deciding to register.

## 4.5 Infringement: the likelihood-of-confusion test

Trademark infringement turns on *likelihood of confusion*. The test: is it likely that a reasonable consumer encountering the accused mark would be confused about the source, sponsorship, or affiliation of the goods or services?

The factors courts apply (slightly varying by circuit; the Second Circuit's *Polaroid v. Polarad* (1961) factors are the most commonly cited):

1. *Strength of the senior user's mark* — fanciful and arbitrary marks are stronger; descriptive marks weaker.
2. *Similarity of the marks* — visual, phonetic, and semantic similarity.
3. *Similarity of the goods or services* — directly competing products produce more confusion than unrelated ones.
4. *Channels of trade* — products sold through the same retailers or to the same consumers produce more confusion.
5. *Sophistication of buyers* — sophisticated buyers (e.g., commercial purchasers of industrial equipment) are less easily confused than ordinary consumers.
6. *Defendant's intent* — bad faith intent to capitalize on the senior mark's goodwill weighs against the defendant.
7. *Actual confusion* — evidence that consumers have actually been confused (typically through surveys or anecdotal reports).
8. *Bridging the gap* — likelihood that the senior user will expand into the junior user's market.

No single factor is dispositive; the analysis is holistic.

Some marks are protected against more than just likelihood of confusion. *Famous* marks (Coca-Cola, Apple, McDonald's, Nike, Google) receive *dilution* protection under the federal *Trademark Dilution Revision Act* (TDRA, 2006). Dilution applies even without consumer confusion — the harm is to the *distinctiveness* of the famous mark itself.

Two flavors of dilution:

*Dilution by blurring* — use of a famous mark in connection with unrelated goods that *blurs* the mark's distinctiveness. A hypothetical "Coca-Cola lawnmowers" — even if no consumer would think Coca-Cola actually makes lawnmowers — would dilute the Coca-Cola mark by introducing a second meaning into the consumer's mind.

*Dilution by tarnishment* — use of a famous mark in unsavory or low-quality contexts that tarnishes its reputation. The classic example: an adult-entertainment company using a famous family-friendly brand name. No likelihood of confusion (no one thinks Disney makes adult films), but the famous mark is tarnished by association.

The trade-off in dilution protection: it buys *broader protection for the most valuable brands* at the cost of *being available only to "famous" marks* (a high bar — the mark must be widely recognized by the U.S. consuming public). Most marks rely on confusion-based infringement; only the iconic brands invoke dilution.

## 4.6 Loss of trademark rights

Three ways trademark rights can be lost.

*Genericide* — the chapter's opening case. The mark loses source-identifying significance and becomes a generic term. Aspirin, escalator, thermos, zipper, cellophane. The remedy: aggressive policing of usage. Trademark holders who see "aspirin" being used as a common noun in newspapers send legal letters demanding "Aspirin® brand pain reliever" or similar. Xerox, Kleenex, Google have all engaged in such efforts. Some critics have observed that the very strength of these brands is what makes genericide a perpetual risk.

*Abandonment* — the trademark holder stops using the mark with intent not to resume. Three years of continuous non-use creates a presumption of abandonment under the Lanham Act. Once abandoned, the mark is fair game for anyone else to register and use.

*Naked licensing* — the trademark holder licenses the mark to a third party without exercising adequate quality control over the licensee's use. The mark loses its source-identifying function (because the licensor no longer controls the quality of goods bearing the mark). The remedy is licensing agreements with quality-control provisions and active supervision.

The trade-off in maintaining a trademark: it buys *indefinite-duration protection* (unlike patents and copyrights, trademarks can last forever in commercial use) at the cost of *active enforcement and policing as a continuous obligation*. Coca-Cola has been a registered trademark since 1893 and is still active because the holder has continuously used and policed the mark for 130+ years.

## 4.7 Synthesis: the Aspirin lesson, fully

Return to the Aspirin case. Apply the chapter's full machinery.

*What kind of mark was* Aspirin? At its 1903 registration, Aspirin was a fanciful or coined term — invented from chemical roots. Strongly protectable in the abstract.

*What went wrong*. Bayer marketed Aspirin so heavily and so successfully — the product became the dominant pain reliever — that consumers came to use the word "aspirin" generically for any acetylsalicylic acid product, regardless of source. Bayer's policing efforts, to the extent they existed, were ineffective. Other manufacturers' products were colloquially called "aspirin" by retailers, doctors, and consumers; Bayer did not enforce strenuously enough to keep the term tethered to its source-identifying function. By 1921, the genericide had been completed.

*Bayer v. United Drug Co.* (1921). Judge Learned Hand, applying the principle that a trademark must function as a source identifier, found that "aspirin" had ceased to do so among ordinary consumers. The mark was canceled. Bayer retained other rights — it could and did continue to market its product under the "Bayer" house mark — but the "Aspirin" name itself was now in the public domain.

*The broader lesson*. Trademark protection is parasitic on consumer perception. As long as consumers see a mark as identifying a particular source, the mark functions and is protected. When consumers see the mark as identifying a category, the mark fails its function and the protection collapses. Marketing that promotes a brand into generic usage has, ironically, been bought too well — the same advertising dollars that made the brand dominant created the conditions under which the trademark would be lost.

The post-Aspirin generations of brand managers learned the lesson. Modern trademark holders enforce against generic usage from day one. They use the brand name only as an adjective, never as a noun ("Coca-Cola brand cola," not "Coca-Cola"). They send takedown notices to dictionaries that list their marks as common terms. They run advertising specifically designed to emphasize the source-identifying function ("the Real Thing," with implied "vs. fake imitations"). Genericide remains a structural risk for any successful brand; the modern remedy is constant attention.

## 4.9 Chapter summary

You walked into this chapter with patents and copyrights. You walk out with trademarks — the third major IP regime, structurally distinct from the other two.

A trademark is a word, phrase, symbol, design, or trade-dress feature used in commerce to identify the source of goods or services. The central goal is consumer protection — preventing confusion about source, sponsorship, or affiliation.

The distinctiveness spectrum sorts marks from generic (no protection) through descriptive (requires secondary meaning), suggestive, arbitrary, and fanciful (strong protection without secondary meaning). Successful brands tend to live at the suggestive-to-fanciful end.

Trademark rights arise from use; federal registration provides nationwide priority and other benefits beyond common-law rights. Infringement turns on likelihood of confusion (multi-factor test, holistically applied). Famous marks additionally receive dilution protection against blurring and tarnishment, even without confusion.

Trademarks last indefinitely as long as they are used as source identifiers. Three threats: *genericide* (the mark becomes the name of the category, as Aspirin did), *abandonment* (non-use with intent not to resume), and *naked licensing* (licensing without quality control).

The single most important idea: trademarks are not property in the same sense as patents or copyrights. They are *use-dependent rights*. The rights exist only while the mark functions as a source identifier and the holder polices that function. A trademark whose holder stops using and enforcing it does not just lose value; it ceases to exist as a protectable mark. This is why successful brand managers spend so much effort on consistency, enforcement, and the careful policing of "use as adjective, never as noun."

The common mistake to watch for: confusing trademark with copyright. Copyright protects the creative expression of a logo (you can't copy the artwork). Trademark protects the source-identifying function of the same logo (you can't use a confusingly similar mark in commerce). The same logo can have both, but each is distinct and operates under different rules.

## 4.10 The Trademark Trial and Appeal Board, briefly

A consequential adjudicatory body within the USPTO worth flagging by name. The *Trademark Trial and Appeal Board* (TTAB) hears two types of disputes: oppositions to pending registrations (filed during the 30-day publication window) and cancellations of issued registrations. The TTAB's proceedings are conducted on a paper record (no live testimony except by deposition); decisions are made by panels of administrative trademark judges.

The TTAB does *not* adjudicate infringement — that remains a federal court matter. It adjudicates registration: whether a mark should issue, or whether an issued registration should be canceled. A party who loses at the TTAB can appeal to the Federal Circuit (the same specialized appellate court that hears patent appeals) or, in some cases, file a civil action in district court.

The TTAB is much used. Major brand owners frequently oppose junior applications they consider too close to their marks; resolution at the TTAB is typically faster and cheaper than full district-court litigation. For small-business mark holders, an opposition or cancellation proceeding can be a substantial unwelcome surprise — the TTAB process, while administratively focused, requires real litigation work. Many opposed applications are abandoned rather than defended, simply because the cost of defending exceeds the value of the registration to the applicant.

A practical note: most opposition proceedings settle. The senior mark holder typically demands the junior applicant withdraw or amend the application (different goods, geographic restriction, modified mark); the junior applicant typically accepts to avoid the cost of defending. The settlement is recorded as a *consent agreement* with the USPTO, allowing both marks to coexist on terms the parties have negotiated. This pattern is the trademark analog of the patent-litigation settlement dynamic from Chapter 2 — most disputes resolve before adjudication, on terms the parties prefer to the cost of full litigation.

## 4.11 Connections forward

Unlike patents (which require disclosure), copyrights (which protect expression), or trademarks (which require commercial use), trade secrets protect *confidential commercial information* — and the protection lasts only as long as the information remains secret. The Coca-Cola formula, KFC's eleven herbs and spices, Google's search algorithm, the secret sauce on a McDonald's Big Mac.
