## 3.1 Opening: The monkey who could not own a photograph

In 2011, a British nature photographer named David Slater set up his camera in a forest reserve in Indonesia. A crested macaque — a black-furred, bearded monkey — picked up the camera and snapped several selfies. One of them, a wide-grinning monkey staring directly into the lens, became famous. Newspapers ran it. Wikipedia hosted it as a public-domain image. Slater objected: it was *his* camera, his preparation, his location; the resulting photograph, he argued, was his copyrighted work.

The U.S. Copyright Office disagreed. In 2014 it issued formal guidance: "The Office will not register works produced by nature, animals, or plants." A photograph taken by a non-human animal was not, in U.S. copyright law, a copyrightable work — because copyright protection extends only to works of *human authorship*. The monkey's selfie was in the public domain. Anyone could use it without permission, including Wikipedia. Slater's commercial expectations evaporated.

Then, in 2015, the animal-rights organization PETA sued Slater on behalf of the macaque (whom PETA named "Naruto"), arguing that Naruto himself owned the copyright. The federal courts dismissed: monkeys cannot hold copyrights either. The case settled in 2017 with Slater agreeing to donate 25% of future revenue from the photo to wildlife protection — though without admitting that the monkey held the copyright. The picture remained in the public domain in the United States.

The Naruto case is unusual in its facts and absurd in its surface details. Underneath, it illustrated several core copyright principles in a single dispute. Copyright requires *human authorship*. Copyright protects *original works fixed in a tangible medium*. Copyright is *automatic on creation* — Slater did not need to register the photo to claim copyright; he did not get one because the photo did not qualify, not because of a paperwork omission. Copyright protects *expression*, not the labor or investment that went into producing the expression — Slater's setup, expense, and effort did not create copyright in a photo he did not himself take.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- *State* what copyright protects and what it does not (ideas vs. expression; facts vs. compilation).
- *Identify* the requirements for copyright protection: originality, fixation in a tangible medium, human authorship.
- *Explain* the duration of copyright (life of the author plus 70 years, generally).
- *Distinguish* the exclusive rights granted to a copyright holder (reproduction, derivative works, distribution, public performance, public display).
- *Apply* the four-factor *fair use* test.
- *Recognize* the role of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) in the modern copyright landscape.

## 3.2 What copyright protects

Copyright is the intellectual property right protecting *original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium of expression*. It is granted automatically on creation; it does not require registration to exist (though registration is a prerequisite for U.S. infringement lawsuits and for certain damages remedies).

The categories of copyrightable works, from 17 U.S.C. § 102:

- *Literary works* — books, articles, software code, scripts.
- *Musical works* — including any accompanying words.
- *Dramatic works* — plays, screenplays, including any accompanying music.
- *Pantomimes and choreographic works.*
- *Pictorial, graphic, and sculptural works* — drawings, paintings, photographs, sculptures, maps.
- *Motion pictures and other audiovisual works.*
- *Sound recordings.*
- *Architectural works.*

The list is not exhaustive — the categories are broad, and new technologies regularly produce works that fit somewhere in the existing categories. Software code is a literary work. A choreographed dance routine, fixed in notation or video, is a choreographic work. A sound recording (of a song) and the underlying composition (the music and lyrics) are typically *two distinct copyrights* — one held by the recording artist or label, one by the songwriter or publisher.

Three requirements for copyright protection.

*Originality*. The work must be original to the author — not copied from someone else. The Supreme Court's 1991 *Feist Publications v. Rural Telephone* decision clarified that originality requires both (a) independent creation by the author and (b) at least a minimal degree of creativity. Bare facts and mechanical compilations (a phone book, an alphabetical listing) lack the required creativity. Selection and arrangement choices that reflect creative judgment can support copyright in a *compilation* even when the underlying facts are unprotected.

*Fixation in a tangible medium*. The work must be recorded — written down, recorded on tape or disk, painted on canvas, captured digitally — in a form sufficiently permanent to be perceived, reproduced, or otherwise communicated for more than a transitory duration. An improvised speech that is not recorded does not have copyright; the same speech written down does.

*Human authorship*. The Naruto case from §3.1. Copyright protects works "of authorship," which the Copyright Office and the courts have construed to require a human author. Works produced by animals, by nature alone, by random processes, or (in the Office's current position) by AI systems without sufficient human creative input are not copyrightable in the U.S. The AI-authorship question is actively litigated as of the mid-2020s; the current Copyright Office guidance requires meaningful human creative contribution for AI-assisted works to receive copyright protection.

What copyright does *not* protect.

*Ideas* — copyright protects the *expression* of an idea, not the idea itself. The plot of "boy meets girl, complications ensue, they end up together" is not copyrightable; a specific romantic-comedy script using that plot is. The Supreme Court's *Baker v. Selden* (1879) established this idea-expression dichotomy.

*Facts* — historical, scientific, biographical facts are not copyrightable. A history book's prose is copyrighted; the facts it recounts are free for anyone to use.

*Useful articles* — purely functional objects (a chair, a wrench, a screwdriver) are protected by patent law, not copyright. The line between expression and function is drawn case by case; the artistic element of a useful article (a sculptural lampbase, a decorative pattern on fabric) can be copyrighted if separable from the functional aspects.

The trade-off in defining copyright broadly: it buys *automatic protection that does not require registration or examination* (a poet's manuscript is copyrighted the moment it's written) at the cost of *thicket-like overlap with neighboring regimes* (patents for function, trademarks for source identifiers, trade secrets for confidential know-how). Practical IP work involves picking the regime appropriate for the asset.

## 3.3 Duration of copyright

Copyright duration in the U.S. depends on when the work was created and who created it.

*Works created on or after January 1, 1978* (when the 1976 Copyright Act took effect):

- Single-author works: life of the author plus 70 years.
- Joint works: life of the longest-lived author plus 70 years.
- Works for hire, anonymous works, pseudonymous works: 95 years from first publication or 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter.

*Works created before 1978* are governed by the 1909 Copyright Act and various transition provisions, with terms calculated from the date of publication and registration. Many older works are now in the public domain because their copyright terms expired.

The 1998 *Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act* (CTEA) added 20 years to all then-existing terms, pushing many works that would have entered the public domain back into copyright. The Act was widely criticized as a giveaway to corporate copyright holders (notably Disney, whose Mickey Mouse copyright would have expired in 2003 without CTEA). The Supreme Court's 2003 *Eldred v. Ashcroft* upheld the constitutionality of the extension. Mickey Mouse's earliest version (Steamboat Willie, 1928) entered the public domain on January 1, 2024, after the CTEA-extended term finally lapsed.

The trade-off in long copyright terms: longer terms buy *stronger investment incentive for creators and publishers* at the cost of *delaying public-domain access to cultural works for generations after the creator's death*. The empirical question — whether 70 years past the author's life genuinely incentivizes more creation than, say, 50 years would — is contested. The duration debate is roughly the copyright analog of the patent-term debate from Chapter 1.

## 3.4 The exclusive rights of a copyright holder

The copyright statute grants the copyright holder a bundle of exclusive rights, listed in 17 U.S.C. § 106:

1. *Reproduction* — to reproduce the work in copies.
2. *Derivative works* — to prepare adaptations, translations, sequels, abridgments, or other works based on the original.
3. *Distribution* — to distribute copies of the work to the public by sale, rental, lease, or lending.
4. *Public performance* — for literary, musical, dramatic, choreographic, audiovisual works: to perform the work publicly. (For sound recordings, the public-performance right is limited to digital audio transmission.)
5. *Public display* — for visual works (and the visual elements of audiovisual works): to display the work publicly.

Each is an independent right that can be licensed separately. A novelist can license the reproduction right to a publisher, the derivative-works right to a film studio (which produces a movie adaptation), the distribution right to a different retailer in different territories, the public-performance right to an audiobook producer.

Two doctrines limit these rights.

*First sale*. Once the copyright holder authorizes the sale of a particular copy, the buyer can resell, lend, or give that specific copy without further permission. This is what makes used-bookstores and libraries legal. The first-sale doctrine applies to lawful copies only; pirated copies remain infringing in any subsequent transfer.

*Fair use* — the next section.

The trade-off in the multi-rights structure: it buys *granular licensing flexibility* (different uses can be licensed to different parties on different terms) at the cost of *substantial complexity* (a work with multiple rights spread across multiple licensees produces a coordination problem when new uses arise — the "orphan works" problem).

## 3.5 Fair use

The most-used and most-litigated copyright doctrine. *Fair use* permits certain unlicensed uses of copyrighted material — for criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, and research — that would otherwise infringe.

Fair use is a defense to infringement, not an affirmative right. The accused user has the burden of proving fair use applies.

The four-factor test, codified in 17 U.S.C. § 107:

*Factor 1: Purpose and character of the use.* Is the use commercial or non-profit? Is it *transformative* — does it add new expression, meaning, or message rather than just reproducing the original? The 1994 Supreme Court decision *Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music* (involving 2 Live Crew's parody of Roy Orbison's "Oh, Pretty Woman") emphasized transformativeness as the central inquiry under this factor. Commercial use weighs against fair use; transformative use weighs in favor.

*Factor 2: Nature of the copyrighted work.* Is the original factual or fictional? Published or unpublished? Factual and published works are more likely to be subject to fair use; creative and unpublished works less so.

*Factor 3: Amount and substantiality of the portion used.* How much of the original was used, and was it the qualitatively important part? Quoting two sentences from a 300-page novel weighs in favor of fair use; quoting the climactic chapter weighs against.

*Factor 4: Effect on the potential market for the original.* Does the use harm the market for the original or its derivative works? This is often the most consequential factor in practice — a use that substitutes for the original in the marketplace weighs heavily against fair use.

The four factors are *non-exclusive* and weighed together. No single factor is dispositive. Courts apply the test holistically, and the same use can produce different outcomes in different cases depending on the specific facts.

A worked example. *Authors Guild v. Google* (2015) addressed Google Books' digitization of millions of copyrighted books and the snippet-display feature that let users see short excerpts in search results. The Second Circuit found fair use: the use was transformative (search rather than reading), the snippet display was limited to non-substitutable amounts, and Google Books did not harm the market for the original books — if anything, it expanded discovery. The decision was a major fair-use precedent for search, indexing, and (now) AI training data, though the AI-training extension is being actively litigated as of the mid-2020s.

The trade-off in fair use: the doctrine buys *flexibility for socially valuable unauthorized uses* (parody, scholarship, journalism, education) at the cost of *unpredictability* (the four-factor test produces case-specific outcomes that are hard to predict in advance). Skilled fair-use analysis is much of what intellectual-property lawyers do for media, education, and software clients.

## 3.6 The Digital Millennium Copyright Act

The 1998 *Digital Millennium Copyright Act* (DMCA) updated U.S. copyright law for the internet age. Two of its provisions matter most.

*Section 512: safe harbors for online service providers.* If a website or platform follows specific procedures — designating a copyright agent, taking down infringing material upon receipt of a proper notice, terminating repeat infringers' accounts — it is shielded from liability for user-posted infringing content. This is the "notice and takedown" system that governs YouTube, Facebook, Twitter/X, Reddit, and almost every modern user-generated-content platform. Without Section 512, hosting a website where users post content would expose the host to potentially crushing copyright liability. With Section 512, the host can operate by responding to takedown notices.

*Section 1201: anti-circumvention.* It is illegal to circumvent technological measures that control access to copyrighted works (e.g., DRM on music, video, or e-books). It is also illegal to manufacture or distribute tools designed to circumvent such measures. The Copyright Office is required by statute to conduct triennial rulemakings in which exemptions to Section 1201 can be granted for specific use cases (e.g., security research, accessibility, repair of medical devices).

The DMCA has been controversial. Its critics argue that Section 512's notice-and-takedown system is abused for content removal that has nothing to do with copyright (DMCA notices have been filed against negative reviews, criticism of corporations, political speech). They argue that Section 1201 has been used to lock down devices and prevent legitimate repair, accessibility, and security research. Defenders argue that Section 512 was the legal foundation that made the modern internet possible and that Section 1201 is the only effective way to enable digital commerce in copyrighted goods.

## 3.7 Synthesis: copyright in the age of AI and platforms

Pull the chapter together against the cluster of copyright issues now in front of courts and legislatures.

*AI and authorship*. The Copyright Office's 2023 guidance requires meaningful human creative contribution for AI-generated works to receive copyright. Pure AI output, with no creative human input beyond a prompt, is not copyrightable. Outputs where the human selected, arranged, and modified the AI material can be copyrightable in those human contributions. The line is being drawn case by case. The Naruto case from §3.1 set the principle (no copyright for non-human authorship); the AI cases are the modern application of that principle to a much larger category of works.

*AI training and fair use*. Training large language models and image generators on copyrighted data has produced multiple lawsuits. The legal question: is training a model on copyrighted text a *fair use* of the underlying works? Plaintiffs argue no — the training reproduces the works wholesale, and the resulting models compete with the works in the marketplace. Defendants argue yes — the use is transformative (training a model is not reading the books), the output does not reproduce the inputs, and the market effect is unclear. The *Authors Guild v. Google* precedent on indexing leans in favor of training as fair use, but the question is far from settled.

*Platform liability*. Section 512's safe harbor has been challenged from multiple directions. Some plaintiffs argue that platforms have become large enough and sophisticated enough that the safe harbor should not apply to all user content they host. The pending *Twitter v. Taamneh* and related cases test the limits of platform immunity in different contexts.

*Term extension and the public domain*. After the CTEA-extended term lapse in the 2020s, a flood of works (including 1928's Steamboat Willie) entered the public domain. The cumulative pressure of long terms, coupled with works whose creators are unidentifiable or unreachable (the orphan-works problem), continues to be a structural strain on copyright policy.

The chapter's vocabulary — original work of authorship, exclusive rights, fair use, DMCA — recurs in each of these. The legal questions get harder; the underlying framework stays the same. Copyright protects original expression fixed in tangible form by a human author for a defined term; everyone else gets to use ideas, facts, and (after term lapse) the expression itself. The chapter is the orientation; specialized practice areas develop the application.

## 3.9 Chapter summary

You walked into this chapter knowing patents. You walk out with the parallel copyright regime that protects creative expression rather than functional inventions.

Copyright protects original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium of expression. The three requirements: originality, fixation, human authorship. Protection is automatic on creation; registration is a prerequisite for U.S. infringement lawsuits and certain remedies but not for the copyright itself.

Copyright duration is generally life of the author plus 70 years for individual works; 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation for works for hire. The 1998 CTEA extended terms by 20 years; the 2020s have begun to see the cumulative impact of those extensions lapse as Steamboat Willie and other works finally enter the public domain.

The exclusive rights are reproduction, derivative works, distribution, public performance, and public display. The first-sale doctrine and fair use limit these rights.

Fair use applies a four-factor test: purpose and character of use (transformativeness), nature of the original, amount used, and effect on the market. The doctrine is flexible by design and is the most-litigated area of copyright law.

The DMCA's Section 512 created the notice-and-takedown safe harbor that makes platform-hosted user content possible. Section 1201's anti-circumvention provisions criminalize bypassing access controls on copyrighted works.

The single most important idea: copyright protects *expression*, not ideas, facts, or labor. The Naruto case turned on this fundamental principle — Slater's effort, expense, and creative setup did not create copyright in a photo he did not himself take. Effort is not a substitute for authorship; copyright recognizes the latter, not the former.

The common mistake to watch for: confusing copyright with patent or with trademark. Copyright protects creative expression. Patents protect functional inventions. Trademarks protect source identifiers. The same product can have all three types of IP protection in parallel, but each is distinct and operates under different rules.

## 3.10 International copyright in brief

Unlike U.S. patents, which require national filing in each country of interest, U.S. copyrights receive automatic protection in most foreign countries through the *Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works* (1886, joined by the United States in 1989). The Convention's 180+ member states agree to protect works originating in any other member state on the same terms as their own nationals. A novel written in the U.S. is automatically protected in France, Japan, Brazil, and 175 other Berne signatories — without separate registration in each.

Beyond Berne, the *Universal Copyright Convention* (1952), the WIPO Copyright Treaty (1996), and the TRIPS Agreement (1994, governing IP under the World Trade Organization) layer additional protections. Practical effect: a published work in any major industrialized country is protected under copyright in most others, with terms and remedies that vary by jurisdiction but that all reach a consistent floor set by international agreements.

## 3.11 Connections forward

Trademarks are different in structure from both patents and copyrights: they exist primarily to prevent consumer confusion about the source of goods and services, they can last indefinitely as long as they're in commercial use, and they require active enforcement to remain valid.
